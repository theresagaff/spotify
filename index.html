3 Ways Quarantine Has Affected My Music Habits

Quarantine has changed us all.
By Theresa Gaffney
Published May 22, 2020 8:00 PM

Here in New York, it’s been just over two months of social distance isolating. Like everyone, I’m going crazy. So I thought I’d be rational for once and take a critical, data-driven look at the one thing that knows my emotions better than I do: my Spotify account.

I’ve listened to over 2400 minutes of music in quarantine. While I used to listen to music at regular, reliable times—on the subway commuting to work in the morning and afternoon—I have no schedule now, and my erratic habits show it.

I took a look at how my habits have changed from the “before times”—the two months of 2020 before the pandemic shut down the city—and the “after times.” The now. And I found three subtle shifts:

1. I’m listening to more ladies.

Of the top twenty musical artists that I’m listening to in quarantine, 70% are females. Before quarantine, only about 60% were female.

2. I’m listening to sadder music.

I created a Sadness Meter: on a scale of 1-10, I assigned each musical artist I listen to a Sadness Rating. 1 is the happy side: your firstborn’s wedding, winning the lottery, dancing like nobody’s watching. 10 is the ultimate sad side: drowning in an ocean of your salty, salty tears.

Now, I’m somebody whose music taste tends to run pretty sad as it is. I like a slow, lilting melody to cry to in the shower, what can I say! But it turns out that quarantine is shifting me just a bit further down that road. Before quarantine, my average was about a 6.2 on the sadness meter, and in 2 months I’ve creeped up to a 6.6.

This may seem negligible, but have you ever been pretty sad, and then the ice cream you were eating to cheer yourself up had a bunch of ice chips in it, and you felt even more hopeless than before? That’s the .2 to .6 shift right there. It’s a slippery slope.

3. I’m much more interested in what certain … friends … are listening to.

My search queries of the past two months are off the rail. Here’s a good old fashioned word could. See those names that maybe don’t look like a musical artist? “katiemyladyy” is a journalism colleague of mine. “Magellan” and “Pfluke” are the first and last name of my high school ex. “Bridget” is an old best friend.

Who am I

Quarantine me is craving those connections, and also digging a bit too much into my past. Is searching your ex on quarantine the lamer version of texting an ex?

These changes are subtle, but they point to my need for the familiar, for comfort and for wallowing in the grief of this moment. We don’t know when things will change, but hopefully I can find some more upbeat bops for a summer of trying to escape the apartment onto my fire escape.
